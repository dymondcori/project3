Drunk Driving Law in the United States

Drunk Driving is a serious problem in the United States contributing to over 10,000 deaths a year.

There are many factors that play into drinking and driving, such as age, gender, location, race and other demographics. Because states determine the law enforcement to keep drunk drivers off of their roads, there are variations in DUI laws across the nation. Some states take DUIs as a much greater offense than others.

The map shows the levels of strictness of DUI law for each state relative to eachother.

It is clear from the coloration on the map that states tend to be more strict in the South and Southwest and more lenient in the North East and North West.

How States are Ranked

Laws vary from state to state on charges and punishment for first, second and third offenses. There are also different laws regarding the consequences for underage drinking and driving. The ranking system for each state's level of strictness takes these factors into consideration, along with other laws regarding drunk driving.

The state rankings decided in a 2015 WalletHub Study take the following into account for criminal penalty and prevention rank:
Criminal Penalty: minimum penalty for first and second offenses, when the DUI is automatically considered a felony, how long a previous DUI factor into penalties for a new DUI, additional penalties for high BAC and a minimum fine for each offense.
Criminal Penalty: when the ignition interlock becomes mandatory, how long the ignition interlock is mandatory, if there an administrative license suspension after arrest, if alcohol assessment and treatment is mandatory, if there is vehicle impound after arrest, the average insurance rate increase after the DUI, a "no refusal" initiative for sobriety tests, sobriety checkpoints and other penalties.

Are State Laws Effective?

The following graph represents the number of DUI related deaths per 100,000 people per state in 2013. The x axis aranges states in order of their strictness ranking from least strict to most strict.

Although every state has a relatively high DUI related death rate, there is a visable negative corrolation between death rate and the strictness of the laws.

States such as North Dakota, Montana, Wyoming, Mississippi and South Carolina stand out on this graph with the highest numbers DUI related death per 100,000 people. Other than South Carolina, all of these states fall in the lenient DUI law category.

While different state's determination and enforcement of DUI laws is important in the fight toward keeping drunk drivers off the roads, the law alone is not enough to put an end to drunk driving.
